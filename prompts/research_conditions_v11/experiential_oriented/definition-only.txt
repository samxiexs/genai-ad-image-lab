Use the source theory's brand concept-image logic as the execution basis. For an experiential concept, the image should translate the brand meaning into the sensory, felt, imaginative, playful, surprising, relaxing, immersive, or cognitively stimulating experience associated with encountering, using, consuming, touching, smelling, tasting, hearing, watching, opening, wearing, holding, preparing, or interacting with the product.

The experiential meaning should arise from the product's immediate encounter and consumption experience. Product-relevant cues such as texture, aroma, taste, freshness, temperature, softness, smoothness, comfort, motion, rhythm, lightness, relaxation, play, novelty, variety, discovery, imagination, or atmosphere can be used when appropriate for the product category. These cues should help viewers imagine what the product feels like in use or consumption, rather than what the product says about the consumer's identity.

The intended viewer inference is not who the consumer is, what status the consumer has, what role the consumer occupies, or what self-image the consumer projects. The intended inference is what kind of sensory pleasure, stimulation, variety, mood, or felt experience the product provides. The product should remain the source of that experience, rather than becoming merely a symbol of taste, achievement, belonging, or personal values. If the image mainly communicates identity, status, role position, self-image, personal standards, or social meaning rather than the felt experience of the product, it does not successfully express an experiential brand concept.
