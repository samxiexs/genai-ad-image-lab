Core question:
What does it do?

The dominant reading must be practical utility. The image should make viewers first infer what concrete external problem the product solves, what practical task it supports, or why it is useful.

Execution guidance for this Function-oriented condition:
Use visible product-function evidence rather than symbolic identity cues or experiential atmosphere. The scene may include product-relevant setup, handling, scale, open or closed state, accessory relation, material detail, storage, protection, or use evidence, but only when these cues clarify utility.

Additional control for all conditions:
The image must support one dominant viewer inference. Secondary visual cues may appear only if they reinforce the target condition. Avoid mixed readings where viewers may equally infer practical utility, identity meaning, and felt experience.

Function-oriented additional guidance:
Do not merely show the product as a clean hero object or neutral product display. Show concrete visual evidence of the task, problem, friction reduction, preparation, protection, organization, or use context that makes the product useful. Functional cues should be externally oriented and task-relevant. Avoid status display, social admiration, luxury aspiration, dreamy sensory atmosphere, or sensory indulgence unless they directly support practical utility.

Function reading test:
A viewer's first response should be: "I understand what this product helps me do and why it is useful."

Failure rule:
If the image mainly communicates identity, taste, belonging, pleasure, immersion, or general lifestyle mood rather than practical utility, generation has failed.
