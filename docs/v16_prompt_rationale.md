# V16 Prompt Rationale: Evidence-Mapped Operationalizations

## Design Rule

V16 distinguishes a source claim from its image-level operationalization.
Runtime controls are permitted only when the following chain can be stated:

`source concept -> image property that makes that concept observable -> bounded prompt instruction`.

An operationalization is not described as a verbatim requirement of a source
article. Where a source does not dictate the exact form, this note labels the
instruction as a transparent research-design choice and explains its boundary.

## Evidence Map

| Condition | Source claim | Image-level implication | V16 control and its boundary |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Product-oriented | Functional needs concern solving externally generated consumption problems; functional positioning emphasizes performance in solving those problems. Park also distinguishes problem-solving specialization from generalization across related situations. | A functional image must make product performance and its relationship to a task inspectable, rather than leaving utility as an unsupported assertion. Homburg et al. treat functionality as a product design's comprehensibility, usability, ability to perform, and communication of utilitarian functions. | Use connected product-centred panels relating an overall product view to grounded features, use actions, or task relations. A panel set is not prescribed by either article; it is a bounded study choice for displaying several inspectable pieces of functional evidence in one square image. It may not invent a function, claim, component, or use. |
| Symbolic-oriented | Symbolic needs concern self-enhancement, role position, group membership, and ego-identification; a symbolic concept associates an individual with a desired group, role, or self-image. Symbolic positioning emphasizes group membership or self-identification. | The product must be legibly connected to a specific social or personal identity, not simply placed in an attractive but semantically empty environment. Homburg et al.'s symbolic design dimension likewise concerns what product design communicates about a person and lifestyle. | Use product design, display, and product-appropriate people or settings as identity cues. Require any person or setting to clarify the product–identity association and keep the identity meaning coherent. This is bounded by Park's warning that mixed concepts can make a brand's basic meaning difficult to identify; it does not authorize generic luxury, success, or demographic stereotypes. |
| Experiential-oriented | Experiential positioning conveys sensory satisfaction or cognitive stimulation and highlights experiential and fantasy aspects associated with consumption. | The image needs a route from product depiction to the experience of engaging with it, rather than relying on a pleasant but product-unrelated atmosphere. Mental imagery research treats imagery as multisensory information represented as a gestalt, and visual product depictions can facilitate embodied mental simulation. | Depict a product-grounded consumption episode from a first-person or close-to-user perspective. Elder and Krishna show that a product depiction facilitating interaction can evoke motor simulation; Hartmann et al. document first-person, invisible-consumer brand images and self-reference. Other participants are allowed only when shared participation is ordinary for the product's use; they must act as co-participants in the consumption episode, not as an identity/status cue. This conditional rule permits a group run for running shoes without imposing a group scene on other categories. |

## Guardrails

- Controls must use only the white-background reference image and product
  metadata; they cannot create new product claims or capabilities.
- A functional panel must show product-grounded evidence, not decorative
  collage fragments.
- An identity cue must be tied to the product and not reduce symbolism to a
  generic status stereotype.
- A participant-centred experiential scene must depict engagement with the
  product; it cannot become a generic lifestyle scene or a symbolic group
  display.
